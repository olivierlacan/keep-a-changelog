# Keep a Changelog 2.0.0 — what changed since 1.1.0

The detailed record of what changed between the 1.1.0 and 2.0.0 pages. The
2.0.0 entry in `CHANGELOG.md` is a synthesized, curated version of this document —
this is the long form for reviewers and translators.

## The most important thing first

**Nothing you have already written becomes invalid.** 2.0.0 adds guidance and
clarifies wording; it does not change the format. Unchanged:

- the six change types — `Added`, `Changed`, `Deprecated`, `Removed`, `Fixed`,
  `Security`;
- the `YYYY-MM-DD` date format and the `## [version] - date` heading;
- `[Unreleased]` and `[YANKED]`;
- reverse-chronological order and links to versions and sections.

There is no migration to perform on your changelog. This is an *upgrade guide* in
the sense of "what's new to know," not "what you must change."

## The big change: from FAQ to integrated guidance

1.1.0 was a short intro followed by a long **Frequently Asked Questions** list.
2.0.0 keeps the friendly opening (`#what`, `#why`, `#who`) but turns the advice
into a structured guide:

- **`#how` — How do I keep a good changelog?** now holds the core guidance as
  named subsections instead of scattered Q&A: `#principles`, `#types`,
  `#breaking`, `#releasing`, `#curation`, plus the questions common enough to
  count as core — `#filename`, `#header` (the `# Changelog` preamble), and
  `#release-notes`. Several of these absorbed adopter-driven conventions: CVE-first
  Security entries and the "keep to the six types" stance (`#types`), inline-vs-
  linked upgrade notes (`#breaking`), the `[version]` compare-link footer
  (`#releasing`), and how to derive release notes without duplicate work
  (`#release-notes`).
- **`#bad-practices` — What makes a changelog worse?** keeps the antipattern list.
- **`#automation` — Changelogs, automation, and LLMs** is a new top-level section
  for the questions a decade of CI/CD and language models raised.
- **`#less-common` — Less common questions** (renamed from "Miscellaneous") and
  **`#about`** collect the situational questions: yanked releases, rewriting,
  large files, monorepos, linking; then standard, scope, contributing.
- **`#references`** replaces the personal "Conversations" footer.

The `#frequently-asked-questions` wrapper heading is gone, along with its first-
person framing. The voice is now plainer and less about the author — see
`docs/tone-and-voice.md` for the guide that drives this.

## The tagline changed

The signature line is new in 2.0.0:

- **Was:** "Don't let your friends dump git logs into changelogs."
- **Now:** "Clearly document the evolution of your projects."

It drives the hero subtitle and the page's meta/Open Graph description, so
translators should translate the *new* line. The old one was git-centric and
leaned on an English idiom; the new one is active, leads with clarity, and is
plainer to translate. Older versions keep the original tagline.

## At a glance

- **9 new sections** of guidance: `#breaking`, `#curation`, `#automation`,
  `#release-notes`, `#large-changelog`, `#monorepos`, `#linking`, `#scope`,
  `#references`.
- **Core advice restructured** from a flat FAQ into `#how` subsections.
- **Several sections revised** in wording (mostly clarifications held back from
  minor releases — see the 2.0.0 changelog entry for why).
- **`#effort` and `#confusing-dates` folded** into `#releasing`.
- **`#github-releases` generalized** to `#release-notes` (any host, not just
  GitHub).
- **Content removed**: the Vandamme gem reference, the "Can changelogs be
  automatically parsed?" framing, the "help me collect antipatterns" aside, and
  the personal podcast footer (kept, synthesized, in `#references`).

## New guidance

### Working with the change types

- **`#types` expanded.** Beyond the six-type list, it now resolves the boundary
  people ask about most — `Fixed` vs `Changed` vs `Security` — with a "was the old
  behavior a bug?" test, an **aside** on why there aren't more types (the reason
  for a change belongs in the entry's wording, not a new type), and direct answers
  for **Dependencies** and **Known issues**.
- **`#breaking` — marking breaking changes (new).** A `**Breaking:**` marker on
  `Changed` / `Removed` entries rather than a new section, plus: name which
  interface your versioning scheme treats as stable.

### Versioning and release structure

- **`#releasing` — Structuring a release (was `#effort`).** Keeps the `Unreleased`
  guidance, absorbs the date-format rationale from the old `#confusing-dates`, adds
  the optional **per-release summary**, and adds that you don't have to use
  Semantic Versioning — CalVer, a plain number, or a date are fine, and
  continuously deployed projects can keep dated entries under `Unreleased`. Now
  also explains the **`[version]` reference-link footer** (resolve each version to
  a `compare/…` URL): this is how a plain changelog provides the tag association
  and "what changed" diff a hosted release page gives you, on any host. `#linking`
  reinforces the same reference-link pattern for issue/PR/commit refs.
- **`#curation` — Curate, don't accumulate (new).** Names the discipline at the heart of
  the project: a changelog records *notable* changes, and deciding which is which
  is human work. Contains the **aside** clarifying that "don't dump git logs" does
  not mean "sort every commit into a type as you make it."

### Automation and the LLM era

- **`#automation` — Changelogs, automation, and LLMs (new).** One section covering:
  - **no separate machine format** — a consistent changelog is parseable *because*
    it was written for people; "this page is the format";
  - **LLM-drafted changelogs** — "machines can draft, but humans curate"; the new
    failure mode is a fluent changelog no one read; includes a portable brief to
    drop into an **`AGENTS.md` / `CLAUDE.md`** (itself an aside);
  - **Conventional Commits and generators** (semantic-release, release-please,
    Changesets, git-cliff) — a commit and a changelog entry are written for
    different readers and don't convert cleanly into each other; type-filtering
    shortens the list but doesn't make that shift; a generated changelog is raw
    material a person still has to curate;
  - **CI/CD** — let it do the mechanics (release-time `Unreleased`→version move,
    format checks, gentle reminders); don't hard-gate every change.

### Maintaining a changelog over time

- **`#release-notes` — changelog vs release notes (new, generalizes
  `#github-releases`).** Different audiences and homes; derive release notes from
  the changelog. Names host release systems (GitHub Releases, GitLab Releases) and
  makes the portability/lock-in case explicit: their notes live in one platform's
  database, don't travel with `git clone`, and are stranded if you change or leave
  the host. Recommends `CHANGELOG.md` as the canonical record that carries the same
  data, with the host's release pages generated from it.
- **`#large-changelog` — very large changelogs (new).** One file is usually fine;
  if you split, archive only stable-old versions and link both ways. Don't prune.
- **`#monorepos` — monorepos (new).** Unrelated projects → independent changelogs;
  a unified product → per-component changelogs *plus* a central one.
- **`#linking` — linking to issues or pull requests (new).** Reasonable and at your
  discretion, but URLs 404 when repos move and PR/MR numbers aren't portable; git
  tags and commit refs are repo-canonical.

### Scope and framing

- **`#scope` — what Keep a Changelog won't do (new).** The non-goals: no growing the
  type set, no machine format/schema/required layout, no becoming a tool or runtime,
  no single-vendor lock-in.
- **`#references` (new).** Replaces the first-person "Conversations" footer with a
  short, synthesized note: how the convention grew (with a link to its own
  changelog) and the podcast, the convention's reach (languages and project count),
  named adopters (NASA, the UK's National Archives, the Wikimedia Foundation,
  Cloudflare, Unity), and its research citations. Written to be easy to translate.

## Revised sections

|---|---|
| `#what` | A changelog is a "curated, chronologically ordered list" (tightened from 1.1.0's wording). |

| `#standard` | Reframed as historical and humbler — KAC doesn't aim to be the one true standard; broadened to any project communicating changes. |

| `#contribute` | Humbler, less first-person; "one carefully considered opinion." |

## Merged / renamed / removed

**Merged**
- `#effort` ("How can I reduce the effort…") → folded into `#releasing`.
- `#confusing-dates` → date rationale folded into `#releasing`.

**Renamed / generalized**
- `#bad-practices` heading "Can changelogs be bad?" → "What makes a changelog
  worse?"

- The personal "Conversations" footer — synthesized into `#references`.

## Presentation changes (for maintainers of this site, not translators)

These don't affect the content's meaning but are part of the 2.0 release:

- **English 2.0.0 is authored in Markdown** (`source/en/2.0.0/index.html.md`)
  instead of HAML, with stable `{#id}` anchors — easier for contributors and
  closer to a real changelog. Other languages and older versions stay HAML for now
  (see Translation impact). Links use **reference style**, with every URL collected
  in one block at the bottom of the file, so the prose stays readable and the link
  list is easy to audit and translate. Fenced code examples use ` ```backticks ```;
  kramdown's GFM parser is enabled for this (its native fence is `~~~`).
- **New stylesheet `v2.css`** — native modern CSS, no framework or preprocessor;
  the brand palette in oklch; logical properties for RTL.
- **Light and dark themes** via `light-dark()` and `color-scheme`, with a header
  switch (`system` / `light` / `dark`). System follows the OS; an explicit choice
  persists in `localStorage` and is applied before first paint to avoid a flash.
- **A table-of-contents sidebar** built in JS from the page's top-level headings
  (the intro cards are excluded, so it lists the real sections): sticky in the
  left gutter on wide screens (with scroll-spy highlighting and a second breakpoint
  that widens it), collapsing to an "On this page" menu on narrow ones that
  dismisses once a section is chosen.
- **A centered "Changelog basics" heading titles the What / Why / Who intro**,
  shown as a card triptych (titles: *What is it?* / *Why keep one?* /
  *Who needs this?*) — a row on wide screens, stacked on narrow.
- **The example changelog collapses** to a tap-to-open disclosure on narrow
  screens and shows outright on wide ones.
- **Heading anchor links** — a `#` revealed on hover/focus to grab a section link.
- **The header keeps to one row on narrow screens** — the language picker collapses
  behind its globe icon below 30rem and reveals the selector when tapped (with
  `aria-expanded`, Escape/outside-click to close); at wider widths the selector
  shows inline as before.
- **The topographic hero pattern and the tree-ring logomark are restored**; the
  pattern is rendered as accessible light contour lines over the deep-ocre base,
  and the brand mark is a theme-colored silhouette that reads in light and dark.
- **Asides vs blockquotes are now visually distinct** — asides are filled callouts
  with an ocre rule (step-aside notes); blockquotes are italic with a gold rule
  (definitions and citations).
- **Accessibility** — the 2.0 design meets WCAG 2.1 AA (verified with axe-core in
  light and dark, including the theme switch, TOC, and hero).

## Translation impact

**Translations are not being converted to Markdown.** Existing language files stay
in HAML, and older versions are untouched. For 2.0.0, the goal is **new
translations authored from the 1.1.0 baseline up to the English 2.0.0** — translate
the new and revised content rather than porting file formats.

- The English `source/en/2.0.0/index.html.md` is the reference for new 2.0
  translations.
- Per `ruby translation_coverage.rb --migration`, each existing translation needs
  the new sections added and the revised sections updated to reach 2.0.0; unchanged
  sections carry over. Run `--migration --details` for the per-language,
  per-section breakdown.
- Section ids are stable across languages, so a 2.0 translation should match the
  English id set: `#basics`, `#what`, `#why`, `#who`, `#how`, `#principles`, `#types`,
  `#breaking`, `#releasing`, `#curation`, `#filename`, `#header`,
  `#release-notes`, `#bad-practices`, `#log-diffs`, `#ignoring-deprecations`,
  `#inconsistent-changes`, `#automation`, `#less-common`, `#yanked`, `#rewrite`,
  `#large-changelog`, `#monorepos`, `#linking`, `#credits`, `#about`, `#standard`,
  `#scope`, `#contribute`, `#references`.
